### ChatGPT: "in proof of stake how is the validator selected"

In **Proof of Stake (PoS)**, the validator is selected **algorithmically**, usually with a mix of **how much stake they have locked** and some form of **randomization**. Instead of miners racing with electricity like in Proof of Work, the network chooses who gets to propose or validate the next block.

#### Core Idea

If you lock coins as stake, you become eligible to validate blocks.
The more stake you lock, the **higher your probability** of being chosen — but it’s usually not a simple guaranteed turn.

Think of it like a weighted lottery:

* 1 coin staked = 1 ticket
* 100 coins staked = 100 tickets

More tickets = better odds.

#### Typical Selection Methods

Different blockchains use different methods:

##### 1. Weighted Random Selection

Most common approach.

The protocol uses randomness and gives larger stakers better chances.

##### 2. Round-Robin / Committee Selection

Used in some systems.

A group of validators is chosen for a time slot or epoch, and turns are assigned.

##### 3. Reputation / Performance Factors

Some networks also consider:

* uptime
* past honest behavior
* response speed
* missed blocks

#### Example: Ethereum

Ethereum PoS uses:

* validators who stake 32 ETH (or via pools)
* random selection for committees
* one validator proposes a block each slot
* others attest (vote) on it

So it’s not just “largest wallet always wins.”

#### Why Randomness Matters

Without randomness:

* biggest staker could dominate predictably
* easier attacks
* centralization risk

So protocols use cryptographic randomness to keep selection fair.

#### Short Version

In PoS, validators are selected by the protocol using a **stake-weighted random process**, often with extra rules for fairness and reliability. More stake improves odds, but does not guarantee every block.

In **Ethereum’s Proof-of-Stake (PoS)** system, cheating doesn’t pay—because the protocol **automatically punishes the validator by destroying part of its stake.** This mechanism is called **slashing**.

Here’s the simple flow:

#### 1. Validator deposits stake

To become a validator, someone must lock **32 ETH** in a staking contract.

#### 2. If a validator cheats

Examples of cheating include:

* **Double signing blocks** (attesting to two conflicting versions of the chain)
* **Proposing conflicting blocks**
* **Surround votes** (a specific consensus rule violation)

These are detectable by other validators.

#### 3. The network slashes the validator

When the violation is proven:

* A **portion of the validator’s staked ETH is destroyed (“burned”)**
* The validator is **removed from the validator set**
* They must **exit the network**

#### 4. Who gets the money?

Important point: **Usually nobody gets it.**

Most of the slashed ETH is **burned by the protocol** (removed from circulation).

However:

* The validator who **submits proof of the violation** (a *whistleblower*) may receive a **small reward**.
* The rest is **permanently destroyed**.

#### 5. Why this works

Because validators have **real money locked up**, cheating becomes extremely expensive. If they try to attack the network, they risk losing their stake.

So in short:

* Validator cheats → **proof submitted by another validator**
* Protocol **slashes the stake**
* **Small reward to whistleblower**
* **Most of the ETH is burned**

No single node or authority “takes all the money”—the system punishes the attacker automatically.

Good question—this is where the **game theory** behind **Ethereum Proof-of-Stake gets interesting.

#### Why Ethereum doesn’t give the slashed ETH to other validators

At first glance you might think:
“Why not reward the other validators with the attacker’s stake?”

Because that would create **dangerous incentives**.

---

#### 1. Prevents validators from trying to trap each other

If slashed ETH went entirely to other validators, they might try to **trick competitors into making mistakes**.

Example:

* Validators could coordinate to cause network conditions that increase the chance another validator accidentally violates a rule.
* Then they would **profit from that validator being slashed**.

By **burning most of the stake**, nobody can make big money from someone else’s punishment.

---

#### 2. Removes profit from attacking the system

If attackers knew someone else would profit from slashing them, it could lead to **collusion attacks**.

Example scenario:

* Two validators cooperate.

### ChatGPT: "what is a rollup"

It is designed to make transactions:

* cheaper
* faster
* more scalable

Instead of Ethereum processing every single transaction individually:

* 10,000 users transact on the rollup

* results are compressed into a batch
* batch data/proofs are sent to Ethereum

So Ethereum secures summaries, not every computation directly.

---

Because many transactions are **rolled up into one batch** before posting to Layer 1.

Think:

1000 transactions → 1 submission to Ethereum.

1. Users send transactions to rollup
2. Sequencer orders and executes them
3. New balances/state are computed
4. Batch is posted to Ethereum
5. Ethereum stores data / verifies proofs

##### 1. Optimistic Rollups

Assume transactions are valid unless challenged.

* Optimism
* Arbitrum

##### 2. ZK Rollups

Use zero-knowledge proofs to mathematically prove correctness.

* zkSync

They help Ethereum scale without sacrificing decentralization as much as putting everything on one faster centralized chain.

* **Rollup** = relies heavily on Ethereum for security/data availability
* **Sidechain** = separate blockchain with its own security model

---

#### One-Line Summary

A rollup is a Layer 2 system that processes many transactions cheaply off the main chain, then posts compressed results to Ethereum for security.

## slide: "Do we need a Blockchain?"

And then the point was, **when is a blockchain application a good application?**

And then there was something by Wüst and Gervais, from the ETH Zurich, and they published a paper, it's linked down there.
And they made this **decision diagram** that allows you to **check if your application requires a blockchain or not**.

- And the first thing is that you need to ask yourself, **do you need to store a state?**
  - If you don't need to store a state, you don't need a blockchain.
  - If you have a transaction, you need to store a state, which is the bank account, therefore you need a blockchain.
  - But if you don't have anything to store, don't use blockchain. That's simple.
- The second thing is, **do we have multiple writers?**
  - No, we have just a single writer. If we are not working in a group or something like that, if this is not relevant for more than one user, we don't need a blockchain. Then you can just store it on your local PC.
- The next question is, **can you use an always online TTP, trusted transaction provider?**
  - If you have something like that, like a trusted transaction provider, don't use a blockchain.
  - That is something that has been criticized, this particular decision. Because somehow what we do is with a blockchain, we replace this particular trusted transaction provider. So that is something that I don't,... I somehow disagree with them.
- The next question is, we need to store a state, we have multiple writers, we don't have an always online TTP, **are all the writers known?**
  - No. Then we need a **permissionless** blockchain. So we have multiple writers, we don't know who is writing, we don't have someone who is doing it for us. We need a permissionless blockchain, which is, Bitcoin, Ethereum.
  - **When all writers are known**, which is,... our community here, imagine we run a blockchain and **we have multiple writers**, yes.
    - We don't **have someone we trust who can do it for us.**
      - No, then we are here.
- Then we say, **are all writers known?**
  - Yes, we are all known, we know each other.
    - Then we go down here, **are all writers trusted, do we trust each other?**
      - **Yes**, no blockchain. If we trust each other, we don't need a blockchain. Because blockchain is for providing trust in an environment where you don't trust everybody.
      - Obviously, **we don't trust each other**.
        - Therefore, **is public verifiability required? Do we need someone else to verify what we put on a blockchain?**
          - **No**, then we make it a **private** permissioned blockchain.
            - So we just run a blockchain among ourselves, nobody is allowed to look into it. We don't even tell anybody that we have a blockchain running, we just use it for our own purposes in order to secure the state that we have among ourselves. Imagine that we just run this blockchain, for example, for a Christmas party where we have our own token to buy the food. We just create this blockchain among ourselves, everybody is running a node on your mobile and that is what we use to create the currency for our Christmas party. It wouldn't make much sense, but that would be this way.
          - Or **if public verifiability is required, yes**, so for example, if we need the ZPA to look into our blockchain because I gave tokens for good questions and good answers, I give tokens away and we run this on our particular blockchain, but the ZPA needs to look into that to check the grades that I give in the end, then we use a **public** permissioned blockchain. It's public, but permissioned, ie. the ZPA can look into it.

So that's their **decision tree** and you will find that in a lot of blockchain papers.
So it's very often it's being checked whether an application is being a good blockchain application or not.

## slide: "Looking for a use case? - From a process perspective"

Some other questions we need to ask **from a process perspective**.

- Do we need a **high level of data integrity?**
  - So look very much for **routine processes** where you need a high percent of data integrity.
  - Grades in university: high data integrity, nobody should be able to change it.
  - Or look for **documentation and proof intensive processes**.
- **Avoid** any **legal or regulated processes** because then you get in trouble with regulation.
  - So, as soon as you deal with money, this becomes a problem.
